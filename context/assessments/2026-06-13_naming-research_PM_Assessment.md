**Session:** 2026-06-13 (parallel brand lane; no Core work touched; no PROJECT_SNAPSHOT/pm-handoff edits — fold at converge). Authority note: sits under D-1..D-7 and the register rules, above the Feb drafts, same as W-11.

**Nick's locked direction (2026-06-13):** lead with `asimtote` as the **company / master-brand name**, with the drop-p `asymtote` as fallback; **software products will not necessarily carry the company name.** Clearance + native-speaker research deferred until the name is locked.

## Verdict

Two independent research sessions, run separately, converged on the same #1 (`asimtote`). That convergence — not either session's weighted integer — is the load-bearing result, and it ratifies Nick's lock. The research is strong on linguistics and honest about its own limits; the real value is three findings, not the scoring tables. **Adopt `asimtote` as the company name pending clearance; carry `asymtote` (drop-p) as the clearance fallback; drop `asymtot`.**

Nick's clarification (company name ≠ product names) is the most consequential input of the round: it **re-weights the package collision down** and **splits the naming problem in two** — the company name is now settled-ish, but the flagship product (the old "HomeSynapse") still needs its own new name, which is a separate, lower-urgency workstream this assessment opens.

## Gold (preserve / fold)

1. **`asimtote` is the only candidate that is simultaneously fanciful and phonetically honest.** Both sessions independently. The "p" in *asymptote* is silent (`/ˈæsɪmtoʊt/`), so `asimtote` spells the source word the way ears actually receive it, while owning no dictionary entry in any language — the Google/Sonos property the whole strategy depends on.

2. **Do NOT borrow a real foreign spelling** — this answers the question you fenced as first-class. `asymtot` (Swedish/Turkish) and `asimtot` (Indonesian/Malay) are *live dictionary words*; borrowing one collapses the mark from fanciful (strongest TM category) to arbitrary, adds a foreign dictionary collision, and buys **zero** worldliness in return. Both sessions reach this independently. It is the reason `asymtot` — one of your two original W-11 candidates — should be dropped, not merely ranked lower.

3. **The radio test is a property of the *concept*, not the spelling** (Return 1B's most candid finding, and the real gold). No asymptote-derived name passes a cold radio test — the silent p, the front-vowel branch (asim/asym/aseem), and the ending all fork. Spelling choice only *minimizes* branches; `asimtote` minimizes them best. You have accepted this cost, which is the right call for a W-1/B2B audience that tolerates coined names — but it carries two standing obligations: pick **one** house pronunciation ("AS-im-tote") and never vary it, and never run audio without a simultaneously visible wordmark.

4. **Dual-register styling is correct and maturation-safe:** "Asimtote" (Title-case: corporate, legal, institutional) + `asimtote` (lowercase: product, CLI, domain), **never lowercase-only** (lowercase-only locks in "toy/indie" and fails the W-3a reversibility test). Folds into the W-6 wordmark spec.

5. **Strategic upside confirmed:** a fanciful mark is the strongest trademark category, which structurally strengthens **D-2 (trademark as commercial control point)**. The rename is a legal *upgrade*, not just a C&D dodge — consistent with the "From Platform to Institution" arc.

## Noise (discount)

- **The per-language scoring tables are informed estimates, not native-speaker panels** (both sessions admit this). Directionally useful — no fatal meaning, slur, or comic reading surfaced across nine writing systems for `asimtote`, and there are quiet positives (Arabic/Hindi "Asim" = protector/boundless). The minor flags are low-risk and shared (Mandarin "si"/死 homophone; PT-BR *assim* = "like this"), and the one real bad-reading (German *tot* = "dead") attaches **only** to `asymtot`, reinforcing its elimination. Treat as a "no landmines" clearance, not a decision input.
- **The weighted-score integers disagree between sessions (35.25 / 37 / 38 …) because they used different weightings.** Don't reconcile the numbers; the ranking *order* is the signal.
- **All "availability" signals except the PyPI collision are flagged "unverified"** (exact `.com` whois, npm/GitHub org, social handles) — these are placeholders for the registrar/attorney checklist, not findings.

## Divergence resolved — the runner-up

The sessions split: 1A's runner-up is the drop-p **`asymtote`**; 1B's is **`asymtot`**. **PM resolution: fallback = `asymtote`, drop `asymtot`.** `asymtot` loses on the sessions' own logic (it's a real foreign word → arbitrary not fanciful → the very thing finding #2 says to avoid, plus the German "dead" reading). Both sessions also flag the drop-p `asymtote` as the *highest paper score* but reject it as "reads like a typo." That objection is weaker for a **company** mark than for a product: a master brand is seen written (legal, footers, contracts) far more than it's shouted in word-of-mouth, and the Flickr/Tumblr/Lyft lineage shows single-letter coinages survive when styled consistently. So `asymtote` is the right *fallback to clear*, not a discard — let the attorney's namespace findings break the tie rather than pre-deciding on aesthetics.

## The package collision, re-weighted

Both sessions verified an **active, on-metaphor** `asimtote` PyPI/GitLab package (University of Cambridge network-config diff tool, v1.1.0 released 12 Jan 2026; its README deliberately invokes "asymptote"). Both treated it as the single biggest practical cost.

**Nick's company≠product clarification drops this from "biggest cost" to "corporate-handle hygiene."** Your flagship product won't ship as `pip install asimtote`, so the developer-namespace collision is largely irrelevant to the product. What the *company* still needs — and should grab on clearance — is the GitHub org, `asimtote.com`, and the social handles. **One residual concern stays on the legal checklist:** Asimptote bv (NL thermal-power *software/SaaS*, plausibly classes 9/42) and the Cambridge tool are both software, so a software/IoT company named Asimtote needs the attorney to clear confusing-similarity vs **Asimptote bv** specifically.

## New strategic item — brand architecture (was buried in W-11)

W-11's PM note leaned "collapse to ONE brand (company = product, the Google model)." Nick's clarification **reverses that toward a parent brand + separately-named products** — which is in fact the *real* Alphabet/Google/Apple model (master brand spanning differently-named products). Implications:

- The flagship local-first home product (old "HomeSynapse") needs **its own new name** — a distinct, still-open decision. W-11 conflated "rename HomeSynapse AND NexSys" into one; in reality NexSys→**Asimtote** (company) is being solved here, and HomeSynapse→**[product TBD]** is not.
- W-7's "a NexSys product" footer model maps cleanly to "an Asimtote product."
- The maturation arc (W-3a) is *better* served this way: a parent brand spans free local product → Connect → Cloud Pro → B2B without any product name becoming a hostage.
- Code namespace likely becomes `com.asimtote.*` (company root, conventional) regardless of product name — a Core-lane refactor detail to schedule at the rename milestone, not decide here.
