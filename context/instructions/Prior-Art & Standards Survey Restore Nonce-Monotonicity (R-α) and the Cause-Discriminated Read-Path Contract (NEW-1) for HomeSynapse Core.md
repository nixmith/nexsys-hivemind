# Prior-Art & Standards Survey: Restore Nonce-Monotonicity (R-α) and the Cause-Discriminated Read-Path Contract (NEW-1) for HomeSynapse Core

## TL;DR
- **Restore nonce-monotonicity (R-α) is BLOCKING, not advisory.** NIST SP 800-38D Section 8 makes nonce uniqueness a `shall` requirement; the Böck–Zauner "forbidden attack" demonstrates that a single (key, nonce) repeat under AES-GCM recovers the GHASH authentication key and enables forgery — so a restore that resumes a counter ≤ a used value breaks both confidentiality AND authentication. Of the two named mitigations, **rotate-the-DEK-on-restore (a)** is the recommended primary contract; carry-the-high-water-mark (b) is a fragile optimization that should only supplement, never replace, rotation.
- **The cause-discriminated read-path contract (NEW-1) is correct and is backed by the closest prior art.** Every event-sourcing crypto-shredding library examined (Axon, patchlevel, Kurrent/EventStoreDB) DEGRADES a key-destroyed event to a default/placeholder value to keep the log replayable, while SQLCipher FAILS CLOSED ("file is not a database") when authenticated decryption fails on data that should be present. These two real-world behaviors map exactly onto CASE (a) intended unreadability → degrade and CASE (b) unintended unreadability → fail-closed.
- **The strongest argument against cause-discrimination is real and must be foreclosed by design:** AES-GCM is not key-committing, so an attacker who can craft ciphertext (or manipulate a key-presence signal) could forge the "intended unreadability" signal and use degrade-and-continue to mask tampering. The chain_hash-over-stored-bytes invariant defends against this, because shredding alters no bytes and invalidates no chain hash — making "key absent" an out-of-band, integrity-independent signal rather than an attacker-supplied one.

## Key Findings

**REC-216 (Mechanism to adopt — nonce uniqueness is a `shall`).** NIST SP 800-38D §8 states verbatim: *"The probability that the authenticated encryption function ever will be invoked with the same IV and the same key on two (or more) distinct sets of input data shall be no greater than 2-32. Compliance with this requirement is crucial to the security of GCM. Across all instances of the authenticated encryption function with a given key, if even one IV is ever repeated, then the implementation may be vulnerable to the forgery attacks that are described in Ref [5] and summarized in Appendix A."* The document's introduction adds: *"a breach of the requirement in Sec. 8 for the uniqueness of the initialization strings may compromise the security assurance almost entirely … this mode of operation should not be deployed unless compliance with this uniqueness requirement is ensured."* Source reliability: **A (primary, NIST SP 800-38D, Nov 2007, current standard; revision in progress as of 2024–2026 but the uniqueness requirement is unchanged).**

**REC-217 (The consequence is authentication loss, not just confidentiality).** The Böck, Zauner, Devlin, Somorovsky, Jovanovic paper "Nonce-Disrespecting Adversaries: Practical Forgery Attacks on GCM in TLS" (USENIX WOOT '16) found via Internet-wide scan **184 HTTPS servers repeating nonces, which "fully breaks the authenticity of the connections,"** and demonstrated a working forgery PoC. This is the concrete evidence that nonce reuse permits GHASH authentication-key recovery and message forgery — i.e., R-α failure is catastrophic, not degraded-but-usable. Source reliability: **A (peer-reviewed USENIX paper + IACR ePrint 2016/475).** This is rooted in Joux's "forbidden attack" (Antoine Joux, "Authentication Failures in NIST version of GCM").

**REC-218 (Deterministic vs RBG-based IV constructions).** SP 800-38D §8.2.1 (Deterministic Construction) builds the IV from a fixed field (device/context identifier) plus an invocation field (counter); §8.2.2 (RBG-based Construction) uses random-bit-generator output. HomeSynapse's per-scope counter-based deterministic 96-bit nonce is the §8.2.1 pattern. Critically, the deterministic construction's monotonicity guarantee depends entirely on the invocation counter never regressing — which is exactly what restore threatens. Source reliability: **A.**

**REC-219 (NIST explicitly flagged power-loss/restore as the hard case for deterministic IVs).** A vendor (Thales) comment in the SP 800-38D public record states: *"The IV construction that is implemented from Sec. 8 above affects the options for recovery from a loss of power. For the deterministic construction, all of the deterministic elements that are necessary to construct the IV would have to be available when power is [restored]."* This confirms the restore/crash-recovery problem is a recognized hazard of the deterministic construction, not a HomeSynapse-specific concern. Source reliability: **B+ (primary NIST-hosted comment document, but a vendor comment rather than normative text).**

**REC-220 (Mechanism to adopt — rotate DEK on restore is the robust primary contract).** Because the only way to *guarantee* no (key, nonce) reuse after a restore is to change one element of the pair, and the nonce counter's restored max may be stale or untrustworthy, the safest contract is to **mint a fresh per-scope DEK on restore** so the entire post-restore nonce sequence is under a new key for which no nonce has ever been used. This makes the restored counter value irrelevant to safety. The crypto-shredding literature corroborates that per-subject/per-scope key rotation is a well-understood, cheap operation. Source reliability: **A (follows directly from REC-216/217).**

**REC-221 (Failure to foreclose — carrying the high-water mark alone is fragile).** Strategy (b), carrying the nonce high-water mark inside the backup, is sound *only if* the persisted max is (i) fsync-durable at backup time, (ii) covered by integrity, and (iii) guaranteed ≥ every nonce ever emitted including any emitted after the backup snapshot but before a crash. The dangerous gap: a backup taken at counter N, followed by writes to N+k, followed by a restore to the backup, resumes at N and reuses N+1…N+k under the same DEK — catastrophic. Therefore (b) must never be used without (a) unless the backup is provably the most recent durable state. **Recommended: (a) as the contract, (b) only as a monotonicity cross-check.** Source reliability: **A (structural argument).**

**REC-222 (Mechanism to adopt — nonce-misuse-resistant AEAD would make R-α moot, at a cost).** RFC 8452 (AES-GCM-SIV, IRTF CFRG, April 2019) specifies AEADs that are *"nonce misuse resistant -- that is, they do not fail catastrophically if a nonce is repeated."* Verbatim: *"some AEADs (including AES-GCM) suffer catastrophic failures of confidentiality and/or integrity when two distinct messages are encrypted with the same key and nonce … Nonce misuse-resistant AEADs do not suffer from this problem. For this class of AEADs, encrypting two messages with the same nonce only discloses whether the messages were equal or not."* Adopting AES-256-GCM-SIV would convert R-α from a blocking correctness requirement into a graceful-degradation property. **Cost/caveat:** RFC 8452 is Informational (not Standards Track), GCM-SIV is two-pass (not "online," requiring the full message before output), and NIST has not approved it under SP 800-38D — so it is not FIPS-validatable today. Source reliability: **A (primary RFC + NIST CSRC pre-draft).**

**REC-223 (Failure to foreclose — assuming GCM-SIV makes you safe to *intentionally* reuse).** RFC 8452 explicitly warns: *"We stress that the nonce misuse-resistance property is not intended to be coupled with intentional nonce reuse; rather, such schemes provide the best possible security in the event of nonce reuse … it is RECOMMENDED that AES-GCM-SIV nonces be randomly generated."* So even a GCM-SIV migration does not license abandoning monotonic discipline; it only removes the catastrophic cliff. Source reliability: **A.**

**REC-224 (Closest prior art for CASE (a) — crypto-shredding systems DEGRADE key-destroyed reads).** The strongest evidence for the cause-discriminated contract: every event-sourcing crypto-shred library examined returns a placeholder and continues rather than failing the read. **Axon (everest-engineering/axon-crypto-shredding-extension):** README states *"Once a key has been discarded, deserialisation will return fields with default values for the field's data type."* (identifiers: `@EncryptedField`, `@EncryptionKeyIdentifier`, `CryptoShreddingKeyService.deleteSecretKey`, `CryptoShreddingSerializer`, `DefaultValueProvider`). **patchlevel/event-sourcing (PHP):** the `#[PersonalData(fallback: 'anon')]` attribute yields *"`anon` for our `name` property if decryption is unsuccessful. With this fallback, our application can still function as expected without crashing due to missing data."* (the fallback is OPTIONAL — *"there is also the option to provide fallback data if desired"*). **Kurrent/EventStoreDB sample (Diego Martin):** the `GetDecryptedOrDefault` method *"will attempt to decrypt the value if the encryption key exists. If it's not, it will use the default value for the data type … and a masked string of `***` when the data type is a string"* (encryption prefix constant `"crypto."`). All three DEGRADE by design to keep the append-only log replayable after erasure. Source reliability: **A/B (A for the primary READMEs/source; B for the blog-hosted Kurrent article text).**

**REC-225 (Closest prior art for CASE (b) — page-encrypted DBs FAIL CLOSED on bad key/auth failure).** SQLCipher's design (Zetetic) uses 256-bit AES per-page with a per-page random IV and a per-page HMAC-SHA512: *"Every page write includes a Message Authentication Code (HMAC-SHA512) of the ciphertext and the initialization vector at the end of the page. The MAC is checked when the page is read back from disk. If the ciphertext or IV have been tampered with or corrupted the HMAC check will cause SQLCipher to report a problem with the database."* In practice a wrong key or HMAC failure on page 1 surfaces as **"file is not a database" (SQLite error code 26)** — a hard fail-closed of the entire database open, confirmed by Zetetic forum threads and the sqlcipher-android issue tracker showing `hmac check failed for pgno=1` → `SQLiteNotADatabaseException`. SQLCipher does NOT degrade per-page; it refuses to open. Source reliability: **A (primary Zetetic design doc + source code + issue tracker).**

**REC-226 (The detection primitive — integrity over stored bytes is what makes cause-discrimination possible).** SP 800-38D notes GCM's useful property that *"The authenticity of the protected data can be verified independently from the recovery of the confidential data from its encrypted form."* HomeSynapse's chain_hash-over-stored-bytes is the architectural analogue: because encrypting a scope or destroying its KEK alters no stored bytes, **a valid chain_hash proves the bytes are intact even when they cannot be decrypted.** This is the signal that distinguishes CASE (a) (bytes intact, key intentionally gone → degrade) from CASE (b) (bytes present but GCM tag fails, or root key missing → fail closed). Source reliability: **A (primary standard + HomeSynapse design invariant).**

**REC-227 (Mechanism to adopt — make "key intentionally destroyed" an explicit, recorded out-of-band fact).** The Axon, patchlevel, and Kurrent systems all treat key destruction as an explicit administrative action (`CryptoShreddingKeyService.deleteSecretKey`, deleting the key row, etc.), not an inference from a failed decrypt. HomeSynapse should likewise persist an explicit shred record (e.g., a tombstone event recording that scope S's KEK was crypto-shredded at time T) so that on read, a decrypt failure for scope S can be matched against a *known* intended-erasure fact, rather than guessed. EventStoreDB corroborates the tombstone pattern: it *"will always keep one event in the stream even if the stream was deleted, to indicate the stream existence and the last event version."* This converts cause-discrimination from heuristic to lookup. Source reliability: **A (corroborated by multiple independent implementations).**

**REC-228 (NIST SP 800-88 validates crypto-shred as a sanitization method, under conditions).** NIST SP 800-88r2 (Sept 2025, supersedes r1 Dec 2014) §3.2 treats Cryptographic Erase (CE) as a Purge-level technique: *"Under the correct conditions and with strong cryptography, these capabilities can be used to sanitize ISM with a purge sanitization technique known as cryptographic erase (CE). At a basic level, CE is based on the sanitization of keys used to encrypt data or to prevent access to the keys that encrypt data."* The conditions (per §3.2.x): the encryption algorithm must meet NIST-approved standards; the key must be destroyed so it is unrecoverable (§3.2.3); the implementation should be validated per ISO/IEC 19790 / FIPS 140 (§3.2.4); and CE operations must be traceable (§3.2.5). This means HomeSynapse's crypto-shred is a recognized sanitization primitive *only if* the DEK/KEK destruction is genuinely irreversible and logged. Source reliability: **A (primary NIST publication; note r2 is recent — freshness horizon Sept 2025; sub-section numbering cross-referenced via a compliance summary against the primary PDF).**

**REC-229 (Mechanism to adopt — age's STREAM construction is the model for counter-disciplined chunked AEAD with a final-chunk marker).** The age format spec (C2SP) encrypts the payload in 64 KiB chunks with ChaCha20-Poly1305, using a 12-byte nonce: *"the first 11 bytes are a big endian chunk counter starting at zero and incrementing by one for each subsequent chunk; the last byte is 0x01 for the final chunk and 0x00 for all preceding ones."* It is explicitly *"a STREAM variant from Online Authenticated-Encryption and its Nonce-Reuse Misuse-Resistance"* (Hoang–Reyhanitabar–Rogaway–Vizár / Hawk–Rogaway lineage). Crucially, age sidesteps the restore problem entirely: *"A new nonce MUST be generated for each file"* and *"The payload MUST NOT be modified without re-encrypting it as a new file with a fresh nonce."* The lesson for HomeSynapse: the final-chunk sentinel byte prevents truncation attacks, and age's "fresh nonce per file" rule is the file-level analogue of "rotate DEK on restore." Source reliability: **A (primary C2SP spec).**

**REC-230 (Failure to foreclose — age/ChaCha20-Poly1305 is not key-committing, same trap as AES-GCM — AND the leading migration target shares the flaw).** The same property that creates the cause-discrimination attack surface exists in age: ChaCha20-Poly1305 *"is NOT key- or message-committing. A ciphertext message can be decrypted under multiple keys without an error."* age mitigates partitioning-oracle risk by checking *"that the body length is exactly 32 bytes before attempting to decrypt it, to mitigate partitioning oracle attacks."* HomeSynapse uses AES-256-GCM, which is likewise non-committing. Critically, the most-discussed extended-nonce migration target does **not** fix this: the C2SP XAES-256-GCM spec states verbatim *"Like AES-256-GCM, XAES-256-GCM is not nonce misuse-resistant, nor is it key-committing"* — so moving to XAES-256-GCM would extend the nonce space without closing the non-committing-AEAD surface underpinning REC-231. Source reliability: **A.**

**REC-231 (The decisive steelman AGAINST cause-discrimination — forged "intended" signal).** The strongest case that cause-discrimination is WRONG: since AES-GCM is not key-committing, an adversary who can influence the key-presence signal could make genuinely-tampered or genuinely-corrupt data *appear* to be intentionally-shredded data, triggering degrade-and-continue and thereby MASKING an attack. If "intended unreadability" were inferred from "decrypt failed AND no key found," an attacker who could delete a key row (or craft a ciphertext that fails to decrypt) could launder corruption into silent degradation. **This is a real and serious objection.** Its force depends entirely on whether the intended-erasure signal is attacker-forgeable. Source reliability: **A (Julia Len, Paul Grubbs & Thomas Ristenpart, "Partitioning Oracle Attacks," 30th USENIX Security Symposium, Aug 11–13 2021, pp. 195–212, ISBN 978-1-939133-24-3; IACR ePrint 2020/1491 — attacks exploit "efficient key multi-collision algorithms … against widely used AEAD schemes, including AES-GCM, XSalsa20/Poly1305, and ChaCha20/Poly1305").**

**REC-232 (Why the steelman is defeated in HomeSynapse's specific architecture).** The objection in REC-231 is neutralized by two HomeSynapse invariants acting together: (1) the chain_hash covers stored bytes, so corruption/tampering of bytes invalidates the chain hash — that is CASE (b), fail-closed, and cannot be disguised as shredding because shredding changes no bytes; and (2) intended erasure is an explicit recorded fact (REC-227), so "key absent" must coincide with a chain-valid shred tombstone to be treated as CASE (a). An attacker would have to both preserve a valid chain_hash AND forge a chain-covered shred record — i.e., break the integrity chain, which is the same bar as defeating fail-closed. Therefore the correct decision rule is: **GCM tag failure on chain-valid bytes → CASE (b) fail closed; decrypt impossible because key is provably, recordedly destroyed while chain_hash validates → CASE (a) degrade.** Source reliability: **A (architectural reasoning grounded in REC-225/226/227).**

**REC-233 (Opposing consideration — steelman for a FLAT fail-closed binary).** The strongest case for always failing closed: it is the simplest contract to reason about and audit, it never risks masking corruption, and it matches SQLCipher's battle-tested behavior. A local-first smart-home store that fails closed on any undecryptable row forces an operator to consciously acknowledge data loss. **Weakness:** it makes crypto-shred (a designed, GDPR-aligned feature) impossible to use without taking the whole store offline, because a legitimately shredded scope would permanently brick every read batch touching it — defeating the replayability that event-sourcing requires. Source reliability: **A.**

**REC-234 (Opposing consideration — steelman for ALWAYS-degrade).** The strongest case for always degrading: it maximizes availability (a single bad row never denies a whole batch), and it matches the event-sourcing prior art (Axon/patchlevel/Kurrent all degrade). **Weakness:** it silently masks corruption and tampering — exactly the CASE (b) failure that fail-closed exists to catch. Notably, even the always-degrade camp shows unease: the Axon extension has open issue #11 ("Improve the API for fields that cannot be decrypted anymore," opened Mar 31, 2021) and Oskar Dudycz (event-driven.io) warns that with disappearing data *"our logic may crash … it's non-deterministic."* This is why neither pure extreme is correct and the cause-discriminated middle is the earned position. Source reliability: **A/B (A for the architectural logic; B for the maintainer-statement corroboration).**

**REC-235 (Signal corroborates the rotation/fresh-key model and the fail-closed-without-key posture).** Signal's Secure Backups are *"end-to-end encrypted and protected by a cryptographically secure 64-character recovery key that is never shared with the Signal service. Without your unique recovery key, no one (including Signal) can read, decrypt, or restore any of the data."* Signal's on-device Android backups are AES-CTR-per-frame with an HMAC, decrypted with a key/IV derived from the backup header — and a wrong passphrase fails the restore wholesale (CASE (b)-style). Signal also refreshes the backup archive daily and treats each as self-contained, sidestepping cross-restore counter-resumption. This corroborates: (i) loss of key → data is *meant* to be gone (CASE a), and (ii) restore is keyed to a fresh, self-contained archive rather than resuming a live counter. Source reliability: **B+ (primary Signal support docs + blog; backup-format internals partly from reverse-engineering write-ups, labeled as such).**

## Details

### Problem 1 — Restore nonce-monotonicity (R-α)

The cryptographic facts are not in dispute and are anchored in primary sources. SP 800-38D §8 makes the ≤ 2^-32 nonce-reuse bound a normative `shall`, and the introduction warns deployment is forbidden "unless compliance with this uniqueness requirement is ensured." The reason the requirement is so strict — and why R-α is blocking rather than advisory — is that GCM's authentication tag is computed with GHASH under a hash subkey H derived from the key; a repeated (key, nonce) lets an attacker solve for H and forge tags. The Böck et al. WOOT '16 paper turned this from theory into 184 real broken HTTPS servers with a working forgery PoC. So if HomeSynapse resumes a scope at counter ≤ a used value under the same DEK, it does not merely leak whether two plaintexts were equal — it potentially hands an attacker the authentication key for that scope.

The write path is already correct (durable, fsync-ahead-of-return, re-init from persisted max on boot). The restore path is the gap, and the design's two named mitigations are not equal:

- **(a) Rotate the DEK on restore** is robust because it makes the restored counter value *irrelevant*: a fresh DEK has a fresh, unused nonce space. This is the recommended contract. It is cheap (one key-wrap operation under the scope KEK) and is exactly the operation crypto-shred systems already perform routinely.
- **(b) Carry the high-water mark in the backup** is an optimization with a sharp edge: if any nonce was emitted after the backup snapshot and before the restore, resuming from the backup's max silently reuses those nonces. It is only safe when the backup is provably the latest durable state — which a restore, by definition, often is not.

A more durable structural fix is to move to a nonce-misuse-resistant AEAD (AES-GCM-SIV, RFC 8452) or an extended-nonce / nonce-derivation construction. GCM-SIV downgrades nonce reuse from "catastrophic" to "reveals only plaintext equality." But it is Informational-status, two-pass (non-online), and not FIPS-approved, so it is a strategic option rather than a drop-in. NIST is actively working this space: its SP 800-38D Rev.1 Pre-Draft Call for Comments (CSRC, document history 01/06/25) names four candidates verbatim — *"a variant of GCM for a block cipher with 256-bit blocks, such as Rijndael-256,"* or *"proposals for deriving GCM key-nonce pairs, such as AES-XGCM [1], Double Nonce Derive Key AES-GCM (DNDK-GCM) [2], XAES-256-GCM [3]"* (citing Filippo Valsorda, "The XAES-256-GCM extended-nonce AEAD," Sept 2024, c2sp.org/XAES-256-GCM). As of June 1, 2026 NIST issued a **second** Pre-Draft Call (comments open through July 31, 2026) proposing a wider 256-bit-block variant **"wGCM"** based on Rijndael-256, superseding the Jan-2025-only framing. Note that XAES-256-GCM, while it extends the nonce, is by its own spec *"not nonce misuse-resistant, nor is it key-committing"* — so an extended-nonce migration alone would not remove the R-α discipline or the REC-231 attack surface. age's STREAM construction (REC-229) is the best illustration of disciplined counter use plus a final-chunk sentinel, and its "fresh nonce per file / never modify in place" rule is the file-level embodiment of "rotate on restore."

### Problem 2 — Cause-discriminated read-path contract (NEW-1)

The survey produces an unusually clean result because the two cases map onto two distinct, battle-tested real-world behaviors:

- **CASE (a) intended unreadability → DEGRADE.** This is precisely what event-sourcing crypto-shred libraries do. The dedicated investigation confirmed via primary sources that Axon ("deserialisation will return fields with default values for the field's data type"), patchlevel (`#[PersonalData(fallback: 'anon')]`, fallback optional), and the Kurrent/EventStoreDB sample (`GetDecryptedOrDefault` → type default, `***` for strings) all substitute a placeholder and continue. The unifying rationale is replayability: the append-only log must remain projectable after a GDPR erasure. EventStoreDB even keeps a tombstone event to mark stream existence after deletion.
- **CASE (b) unintended unreadability → FAIL CLOSED.** This is precisely what SQLCipher does: an HMAC failure or wrong key on page 1 produces "file is not a database" and refuses to open. It never degrades a page silently, because a silent degrade would mask corruption or tampering.

The architectural enabler that lets HomeSynapse have *both* behaviors safely is the chain_hash-over-stored-bytes invariant. Because encrypting a scope or destroying its key changes no stored bytes, the integrity chain stays valid through shredding. So the decision rule is well-defined:

1. Decrypt fails AND chain_hash validates AND an explicit, chain-covered shred record exists for the scope → **CASE (a), degrade the row to a degraded-event placeholder, continue.**
2. GCM authentication fails on chain-valid bytes (no shred record), OR the root key is missing/corrupt at boot, OR chain_hash itself fails → **CASE (b), fail closed.**

The most serious objection (REC-231) is that AES-GCM is non-committing, so an attacker might forge the "intended" signal — a concern made concrete by the partitioning-oracle attacks of Len, Grubbs & Ristenpart (USENIX Security '21), which build key multi-collisions against exactly AES-GCM and ChaCha20/Poly1305. The defense (REC-232) is that in HomeSynapse the intended signal is not "decrypt failed" — it is "decrypt is impossible because a recorded, chain-covered shred destroyed the key, while the bytes still pass the integrity chain." Forging that requires breaking the integrity chain, which is the same work as defeating fail-closed. This is why cause-discrimination is *earned* here specifically: it relies on the chain_hash being independent of encryption, which is a stated HomeSynapse invariant, not a generic assumption.

## Recommendations

**Stage 1 — Land R-α as BLOCKING (route to the app-bootstrap milestone).**
1. Adopt **rotate-DEK-on-restore (strategy a)** as the binding restore contract. On any restore, mint a fresh per-scope DEK (re-wrapped under the scope KEK) before resuming any encryption in that scope. This makes the restored counter value irrelevant to safety.
2. Treat **carry-the-high-water-mark (strategy b)** only as a *defense-in-depth cross-check* (assert resumed counter ≥ carried max), never as the sole guarantee.
3. Add a boot-time invariant check that refuses to encrypt in any scope until either a fresh DEK is installed or the persisted counter is proven ≥ all previously emitted nonces.
- **Threshold that changes this:** if HomeSynapse migrates to AES-256-GCM-SIV or an approved nonce-misuse-resistant AEAD, R-α downgrades from blocking to advisory and the rotate-on-restore contract can be relaxed to "recommended." Note that a *mere* extended-nonce move (e.g., XAES-256-GCM) does NOT meet this threshold — it is not misuse-resistant by its own spec.

**Stage 2 — Land NEW-1 as the cause-discriminated contract (route obligations to a crypto-shred work unit + app-bootstrap milestone).**
1. **CASE (a) → degrade:** when decrypt is impossible due to a recorded, chain-valid crypto-shred, emit a degraded-event placeholder and continue the read. (Route to the crypto-shred work unit.)
2. **CASE (b) → fail closed:** when GCM authentication fails on chain-valid bytes, or the root key is missing/corrupt at boot, or chain_hash fails — fail the whole read batch closed and surface a distinct, loud error. (Route to app-bootstrap milestone.)
3. **Make intended erasure explicit:** persist a chain-covered shred tombstone (scope, timestamp, reason) so CASE (a) is a lookup, never an inference from a bare decrypt failure. (Crypto-shred work unit.)
4. **Signal by cause:** the degraded-event placeholder MUST carry the cause (intended-shred vs unknown) so downstream projections can distinguish "data designed to be gone" from "data we failed to read."
- **Threshold that changes this:** if a future audit shows the shred-tombstone path is itself forgeable without breaking the chain (e.g., a chain-hash weakness), collapse to flat fail-closed until fixed.

**Stage 3 — Strategic hardening.**
1. Evaluate a committing-AEAD or Encrypt-then-MAC wrapper (encryption and authentication keys derived from the same IKM, with a ≥256-bit collision-resistant hash-based MAC — the construction the committing-AEAD drafts identify as committing) to close the non-committing-AEAD attack surface that underpins the REC-231 objection.
2. Mirror Signal's "fresh, self-contained archive per backup cycle" model to avoid cross-restore counter resumption entirely.
3. Track the SP 800-38D Rev.1 process (NIST first pre-draft Jan 2025; second pre-draft proposing Rijndael-256-based "wGCM" with comments open through July 31, 2026) for an approved extended-nonce/derivation or 256-bit-block mode.

## Caveats

- **Evidence AGAINST the cause-discriminated design (honesty section).** The non-committing nature of AES-GCM is a genuine structural weakness (REC-230/231): without the chain_hash invariant, cause-discrimination would be unsafe because the "intended" signal could be forged. The design is only as strong as the chain_hash's independence from encryption. If that invariant is ever violated (e.g., chain_hash computed over plaintext, or over bytes that shredding does change), the entire cause-discrimination argument collapses and flat fail-closed becomes mandatory. The Axon extension's own open issue #11 and Dudycz's "logic may crash / non-deterministic" caution show that even degrade-favoring practitioners regard the read-after-shred API as unsettled.
- **Gaps that could not be closed.** (i) AxonIQ's *commercial* Data Protection module read-path behavior is not documented in primary sources (only the open-source everest-engineering extension is); the degrade finding is firmly established for the OSS extension, patchlevel, and Kurrent, but the commercial module's exact exception/flag behavior is unverified. (ii) Signal's backup-format internals (AES-CTR per frame, HMAC) come partly from reverse-engineering write-ups (xeals/signal-back, jhnet.co.uk), labeled as aggregator/community-grade; the user-facing key-loss-means-data-gone behavior is from primary Signal docs. (iii) The exact §3.2 sub-section numbering of SP 800-88r2's CE conditions is cited via a secondary compliance summary cross-referenced against the primary r2 PDF; the verbatim CE definition is from the primary PDF.
- **Freshness horizons.** SP 800-38D is the Nov 2007 final (revision in progress — first pre-draft Jan 2025, second pre-draft June 2026; uniqueness requirement unchanged). RFC 8452 is April 2019. SP 800-88r2 is Sept 2025 (recent; supersedes r1). The age spec, SQLCipher design, and the crypto-shred library behaviors were confirmed as of June 2026. Böck et al. is 2016; the Len–Grubbs–Ristenpart partitioning-oracle work is 2021.
- **Aggregator quarantine.** Grokipedia (crypto-shredding), db-browser.github.io (SQLCipher troubleshooting), and various blog tutorials were used only for orientation, not load-bearing claims; every load-bearing claim above traces to a primary RFC, NIST publication, official design doc, source repository, or maintainer statement.
- **Scope discipline.** This survey recommends the read-path contract and surveys the restore prior art; it does not mint binding milestone/type/contract obligations and makes no market/positioning claims, per the engineering-register frame. Findings are numbered REC-216 through REC-235 and routed to two disposition buckets: a **crypto-shred work unit** (REC-224, REC-227, and the degrade half of REC-232/Stage-2 items 1, 3, 4) and an **app-bootstrap milestone** (REC-216–REC-223 restore/nonce contract, REC-225/226 fail-closed detection, and the fail-closed half of Stage-2 item 2).
