## D-1 (REC-172) — Matter posture: economics + trademark fence → file 2 (Tier-3 section)

## D-2 (REC-173 + 183) — Governance credibility; license re-affirmed → file 2 (licensing section)

**Insert-ready draft:**
> **The fork-pattern evidence re-affirms Apache 2.0.** Every recent ecosystem fork that mattered (the relicensing case studies) was triggered by a move AWAY from OSI-permissive terms — the exact move our locked Apache 2.0 decision makes structurally impossible. The license is settled; what the evidence adds is that **governance posture is the remaining credibility surface**: (a) no CLA that enables future relicensing — inbound = outbound; (b) the NexSys trademark, not the license, is the commercial control point (the Matter fence above is the same instrument); (c) foundation-optionality is kept open deliberately (see the institution report) but never promised early. And the corollary the climbdown evidence teaches (REC-183): trust lost by a licensing wobble is not restored by reverting it — which is why this paragraph is written BEFORE launch, not after a controversy.

**Provenance:** R15 REC-173 (verdict corrected in-Project: license CONFIRMED, governance refined) + REC-183 (folded into the same row at assessment).

## D-3 (REC-178) — OHF governance model input → file 3 (institution-building section)

**Insert-ready draft:**
> **The Open Home Foundation is the governance gold standard to study — and the bar.** OHF demonstrated the full pattern: assets (trademarks, code, domains) held by a Swiss non-profit that cannot be acquired, a commercial arm (Nabu Casa) funding development through services rather than gatekeeping, and an explicit "protected from corporate hijacking, buyouts, and abandonment" promise. When NexSys reaches the institution phase, this is the reference architecture — with one deliberate difference we keep: self-funding from revenue layers 2–4 rather than donation-dependence, which OHF's model tolerates but our no-VC principle already demands.

**Provenance:** R15 REC-178 (CONFIRMED; synergy with file 2's no-VC/self-funding principle noted at assessment).

## D-4 (REC-180) — Segment messaging rule → files 1 + 3 (positioning/messaging sections)

**Insert-ready draft:**
> **Messaging rule — privacy is a segment lead, not THE lead.** The mass-market buyer is privacy-indifferent at purchase time (the evidence is consistent: convenience and "it just works" decide). Therefore: for the mainstream-homeowner expansion, lead with **reliability, works-together, and plug-and-play** (Battlefields 1 and 6, plus the Hub); privacy-first framing LEADS only for the prosumer and EU segments where it demonstrably converts (and where the Data Act/CRA tailwinds give it dated, named-regulation teeth). Privacy never disappears from the story — it stops being the headline where it doesn't sell. This is a messaging rule, NOT a scope change: we do not scope out convenience buyers.

**Provenance:** R15 REC-180 (NARROWED at assessment from "scope out" to the messaging rule — file 3 explicitly pursues the mainstream expansion).

## D-6 (REC-185) — Unsized-segment risk note → files 3 + 4 (projection sections)

**Insert-ready draft:**
> **Known unknown: the prosumer segment is unsized.** No discrete sizing of the self-hosting prosumer smart-home segment exists in the public record (honestly declared by our own research). Every projection in this document inherits that uncertainty; treat the conversion assumptions, not the pool size, as the sensitivity lever — and revisit when our own funnel produces first-party numbers.

**Provenance:** R15 REC-185 (CONFIRMED; RQ5 declared PARTIALLY-INCOMPLETE — a real-world gap, not a research failure).
