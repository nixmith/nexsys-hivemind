# Consult Scorecard (30 minutes; memo sent ahead)

**Attorney:** ______________ **Date/time:** ______________ **Conflicts answer received in writing? Y / N**

Open the call: *"You have the memo — I'd like to spend the 30 minutes on your read of it, your relevant experience, and fees. I'll take notes and I'm talking to two other firms this week."* (Saying so is normal and keeps everyone honest.)

## The six questions — and how to score the answers (1–5 each)

### Q1. "How many US trademark applications does your practice file per year, and is prosecution the core of your practice?"
- **GOOD (4–5):** A concrete number without hesitation ("about 150–400 a year"), prosecution-first framing, examples in consumer products/software. Volume with specificity = docketing systems and examiner pattern-knowledge you're renting.
- **OK (3):** Modest volume but deep specialization ("60–80/yr, all trademark, I argue the hard ones myself").
- **BAD (1–2):** Vague ("we do a lot"), litigation-first identity, or trademark as a side dish to patents/corporate.

### Q2. "Tell me about §2(d) likelihood-of-confusion refusals you've argued — and your outcomes. Mine may hinge on a goods-gap and crowded-field argument."
- **GOOD:** Specific war stories; unprompted use of the exact concepts your case runs on (relatedness of goods, channels of trade, crowded field, coexistence/consent agreements); honest about losses and what drove them. Bonus: they've handled §66(a)/Madrid-designated adversaries.
- **OK:** Fewer stories but a correct, fluent explanation of how they'd argue TAMORO v. TOMORO on your facts.
- **BAD:** "Refusals are rare if you search well" (dodges the question), guarantees of overcoming, or experience limited to non-substantive OAs (punctuation/ID fixes only).

### Q3. "I plan direct national filings in Japan, Australia, UK, and EU inside the Paris window — not Madrid. Can you run that through your own foreign-associate network, and what does each country roughly cost through you?"
- **GOOD:** Has a real network ("our JP associate is X, we file there monthly"), can sketch per-country costs from memory, and can articulate WHY direct beats Madrid for you unprompted (broader UK/EU specs, no central-attack dependency, no narrow-US-ID import) — that's the memo's own logic; hearing it back unprompted is a very strong signal. Knows the JP mechanics (first-to-file, resident agent, maybe katakana タモロ).
- **OK:** Smaller network but direct-filing capable and quotes within a day.
- **BAD:** Madrid-by-default reflex ("Madrid is cheaper, why bother?") without engaging your reasons; no network ("we'd find someone"); or foreign = a white-label service they resell.

### Q4. "What are your flat fees for: the comprehensive search + written opinion; the US §1(b) filing; and an office-action response if we draw one?"
- **GOOD:** Immediate numbers or a same-day written quote, phase-by-phase, government fees separated out. Benchmarks (§2 of the instruction, re-verify at engagement): search + opinion $1–3k; US filing $500–1.5k flat + gov't fees; substantive OA response often $1.5–3.5k. Your Phase-1 guardrail: **$2,500–4,500 all-in including government fees; walk at $8k+.**
- **OK:** Flat for search/filing, capped-hourly for OA responses (common and reasonable).
- **BAD:** "It depends" with no ranges, hourly-only uncapped, or bundling you into a big retainer before the search.

### Q5. "Who actually does the work — the search analysis, the opinion, the OA response — and what's the turnaround?"
- **GOOD:** A named attorney (ideally the person on the call) reads the search and signs the opinion; search 1–2 weeks; filing days after a green opinion. Paralegals doing data entry is normal — analysis by a non-attorney is not.
- **BAD:** Can't say who; opinion is a template; search outsourced with no attorney analysis layered on; weeks of turnaround with no reason.

### Q6. THE PUSHBACK TEST — "You've seen Part 11 of the memo. What do you disagree with? What did I get wrong or miss?"
This is the most diagnostic five minutes of the call. The memo deliberately ends by inviting attack (Part 11 Q10).
- **GOOD:** Engages substantively and disagrees with something concrete — e.g., pushes back on the direct-vs-Madrid preference with actual reasoning, flags the Class-42 "AI for residential applications" wording as too hot while IR 1826591 is live (Q5), argues the UK file-now-vs-wait-for-2029 question differently (Q4), questions the entity/chain-of-title sequencing (Q6), or names a risk the memo underweights (e.g., the crowded-field evidence needs building, common-law search gaps). Disagreement quality = the product you're buying.
- **OK:** Agrees with the thrust but adds material refinements and can defend them.
- **BAD — very bad:** "This looks great, we can file today" (a rubber stamp — you already have a rubber stamp, it's called the memo) — or waves the memo off without demonstrating they read it. Either way you learned they won't protect you from yourself.

## Red flags — END THE CALL (any one of these; no score can save it)
1. **Guarantees registration** (no honest attorney can).
2. **Will file without a comprehensive search** on these facts (a live near-identical senior mark).
3. **Fee vagueness** after being asked directly.
4. **Non-attorney "consultants"** doing legal analysis.
5. **Hourly-only, uncapped**, on what the market prices flat.
